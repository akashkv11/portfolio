About Me

The Beginning: A Curious Click

Hi there! I'm Akash, and like many developers, my journey started with pure curiosity. I was scrolling through Facebook one day during my college years when I accidentally hit F12. Suddenly, this mysterious panel opened up showing all this code. Out of curiosity, I found where it said "Facebook" in the HTML and changed it to my own name. When I saw my edit appear on the page, I literally sat there staring at the screen in disbelief.

That moment changed everything. I realized that websites weren't just static things that existed—they were built by people like me, using code I could actually understand and modify.

The Early Days: From Notepad to Passion

What really hooked me was discovering how accessible web development was. Unlike other programming languages that needed complex IDEs and environments, I could create a file with a .html extension, write some code in notepad, and instantly see results in any browser. No installation wizards, no configuration files—just pure, immediate feedback.

I spent hours in those early days just experimenting. I'd write a simple HTML page, add some CSS styling, maybe a bit of JavaScript for interactivity, and watch my ideas come to life. That immediate visual feedback loop became addictive. I wasn't just writing abstract code—I was building interfaces, creating experiences.

During my high school and college computer science courses, while my classmates were focused on algorithms and data structures (which I appreciate now!), I was always drawn to anything that had a visual output. I wanted to see my code work in the real world, not just pass test cases.

The API Discovery: Building My First Real Project

One of my most memorable early projects happened when I stumbled across a list of public APIs online. I was amazed—here were these services that could provide me with real data! I found several image-fetching APIs with different categories and thought, "What if I could build a gallery app that pulls images from these APIs?"

So I did. I built an image gallery where users could select different categories—nature, technology, abstract art—and the app would dynamically fetch and display images in a responsive grid layout. It wasn't just a static webpage anymore; it was a living, breathing application that interacted with real data sources.

The best part was showing it to friends and family. Watching their faces when they clicked a category and saw fresh images populate instantly—that's when I knew I had found my calling. I wasn't just coding; I was creating experiences that people could enjoy and interact with.

My Development Philosophy

For me, the most exciting part of programming isn't solving abstract problems—it's seeing the output as something tangible, something that connects with the real world. I love that moment when your code transforms from lines of text into a living interface that people can actually use.

This is why I gravitated toward full-stack development. I enjoy understanding the complete flow: how data moves from the database through the backend APIs, gets processed and transformed, then renders beautifully on the frontend. There's something satisfying about being able to trace a user's click all the way through the entire system architecture.

I'm passionate about writing clean, maintainable code that follows best practices like SOLID principles. But I also believe that great code should serve great user experiences. Technical excellence and user satisfaction should go hand in hand, not compete with each other.

Professional Journey: Growth at Reon Technologies

At Reon Technologies, I've had the opportunity to work on challenging, real-world applications with actual users and business impact. Over my 2+ years here, I've been able to deliver measurable improvements: 30% faster frontend performance and 25% higher team productivity through code reviews, knowledge sharing, and streamlining our development processes.

But beyond the metrics, what I'm most proud of is how I've grown as a mentor and team player. I've helped onboard new developers, led code review sessions, and contributed to improving our Agile processes and CI/CD pipelines. There's something deeply satisfying about not just writing better code yourself, but helping your entire team level up.

I've worked extensively with the MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React, Node.js), but I've also explored other technologies based on project needs. I believe in choosing the right tool for the job, not just defaulting to what you know.

The Learning Never Stops

One thing that drives me is the constant evolution of web development. There's always a new framework, a better way to solve an old problem, or an emerging technology that opens up new possibilities. I love diving into new programming languages, frameworks, and technologies—not just to add them to my resume, but because each one teaches you to think about problems differently.

I maintain a habit of building small experimental projects to test out new technologies. Whether it's trying out a new React feature, experimenting with a different database solution, or exploring the latest CSS capabilities, I believe hands-on experimentation is the best way to truly understand new tools.

Mentoring: Sharing the Journey

Teaching others has become a significant part of my development journey. Whether it's helping teammates debug complex issues, explaining architectural decisions to junior developers, or sharing knowledge about both technical and non-technical topics, I find mentoring incredibly rewarding.

There's something special about that moment when you're explaining a concept and you see the lightbulb go on in someone else's eyes—it reminds me of my own "aha!" moment with that Facebook inspect element. I believe knowledge becomes more valuable when it's shared, and helping others grow makes the entire development community stronger.

Beyond the Screen: Life, Football, and Balance

When I step away from the computer, you'll most likely find me on a football pitch. Football is my other passion—the strategic plays, the teamwork, the split-second decisions, and the satisfaction of executing the perfect move. In many ways, football and coding share similarities: they both require strategic thinking, collaboration, and the ability to adapt quickly when things don't go according to plan.

I consider myself an ambivert, which serves me well in development. I enjoy focused, deep-work coding sessions where I can dive into complex problems, but I also thrive in collaborative environments where we're brainstorming solutions, conducting code reviews, or planning architecture decisions as a team.

I'm someone who finds humor in both the frustrations and victories of coding life—from spending hours debugging only to find a missing semicolon, to the satisfaction of finally getting a complex feature working perfectly. I think maintaining a sense of humor is essential in this field where things break in mysterious ways and requirements change at the last minute.

The people who matter most to me are my family and my girlfriend, who've supported my passion for technology and my continuous learning journey. They've listened patiently to my excited explanations of new projects, celebrated my career victories, and provided perspective during challenging times.

What's Next: My Vision

I'm seeking a role where I can continue pushing the boundaries of what's possible with web technology. I want to work with a team that values both technical excellence and user experience, where I can contribute to products that make a real difference in people's lives.

I'm excited about opportunities to work with emerging technologies, tackle complex architectural challenges, and continue growing as both a developer and a mentor. I believe the best software is built by teams that combine technical skill with genuine care for the users they serve.

My ideal role would allow me to contribute to meaningful projects while continuing to learn, grow, and help others along their development journeys. After all, that's what got me started in the first place—curiosity, learning, and the desire to build something that matters.
